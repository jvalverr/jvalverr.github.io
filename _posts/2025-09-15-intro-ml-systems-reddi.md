I rarely get impressed by a textbook, but *Introduction to Machine Learning Systems* by Vijay Janapa Reddi (2025) left me with that feeling.

## What makes it different

What struck me most is how it turns complex, often fragmented concepts into a coherent vision of what AI systems really are: **not just models, but end-to-end architectures that bring intelligence into production.**

The book explains why the real challenge is no longer training models in isolation, but building systems that balance efficiency, scalability, and responsibility. It covers everything from performance engineering to trustworthy AI, and even explores how LLMs, agents, and generative systems reshape the design of modern ML pipelines.

And it does so with clarity and stunning diagrams that make you see the forest and the trees — a rare feat in technical writing.

## Why it matters

For me, this is more than a textbook. It's a blueprint for the next generation of AI engineers — those who will transform today's algorithms into tomorrow's real-world systems.

If you're building AI that needs to scale, integrate, and last beyond the demo phase, this is essential reading.
